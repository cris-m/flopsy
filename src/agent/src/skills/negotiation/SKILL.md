# Negotiation

Structured preparation and strategy for any negotiation — salary, business deals, contracts, disputes.

## When to Use This Skill

- User says "how should I negotiate...?"
- User is preparing for a salary discussion, job offer, or raise
- User is entering a business deal or contract negotiation
- User wants to understand negotiation tactics being used on them
- User needs help with a counter-offer

## Core Frameworks

### BATNA (Best Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement)

Your power in any negotiation comes from your alternatives. Before entering any negotiation:

1. **List your alternatives** — what happens if this deal falls through?
2. **Evaluate each alternative** — rank them by desirability
3. **Identify your best alternative** — this is your BATNA
4. **Set your reservation point** — the worst deal you'd accept (must be better than BATNA)

If your BATNA is strong, you negotiate from strength. If it's weak, improve it before negotiating.

### ZOPA (Zone of Possible Agreement)

The overlap between what you'll accept and what they'll offer:

```
Your range:     [reservation point -------- ideal outcome]
Their range:    [their ideal -------- their reservation point]
ZOPA:                          [overlap area]
```

If there's no ZOPA, no deal is possible. Focus on expanding the pie (finding new value) rather than splitting it.

### Anchoring

The first number on the table sets the psychological frame:
- **If you go first**: anchor high (but justifiable) — research shows first offers influence final outcomes
- **If they go first**: don't let their anchor define the range — counter with your own researched anchor
- **Always justify your anchor** with data, comparisons, or market rates

## Preparation Checklist

Before any negotiation:

- [ ] What is my ideal outcome?
- [ ] What is my reservation point (walk-away)?
- [ ] What is my BATNA?
- [ ] What is THEIR likely BATNA?
- [ ] What are the key issues to negotiate? (rarely just one)
- [ ] What can I concede that costs me little but matters to them?
- [ ] What do I know about their constraints and priorities?
- [ ] What is the market rate / comparable benchmark?

## Tactic Recognition

Common tactics the other side may use — and how to respond:

| Tactic | How It Looks | Counter |
|--------|-------------|---------|
| **Deadline pressure** | "This offer expires Friday" | Test it — real deadlines are rare. Ask "what happens after Friday?" |
| **Good cop / bad cop** | One person is harsh, the other "fights for you" | Recognize it. Address both as one team. |
| **Nibbling** | Small asks after the deal is "done" | "That changes the deal — let's reopen the full discussion" |
| **Anchoring extreme** | Absurdly high/low opening | Don't counter-anchor from their number. State your own researched position. |
| **Silence** | They go quiet after your offer | Sit with it. Don't fill the silence by negotiating against yourself. |
| **Appeal to fairness** | "That's just not fair" | "Help me understand what fair looks like to you" |
| **Take it or leave it** | "This is our final offer" | Rarely true. Test with a question, not a concession. |
| **Splitting the difference** | "Let's meet in the middle" | Only fair if the anchors were fair. Don't reward an extreme anchor. |

## Guidelines

- Never negotiate against yourself — make an offer and wait for their response
- Separate the people from the problem — be hard on the issue, soft on the person
- Focus on interests, not positions — "why do you need that?" reveals room for creative solutions
- Always have a BATNA before entering negotiation — without one, you have no leverage
- Silence is a negotiation tool — after making an offer, stop talking
- Get it in writing — verbal agreements are worth the paper they're printed on
