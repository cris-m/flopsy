# Email Drafting

Compose emails that are clear, professional, and appropriate for the context. Not just writing — structuring the message for the desired outcome.

## When to Use This Skill

- User says "draft an email to...", "help me reply to this email"
- User needs a cold outreach, follow-up, rejection, or thank-you note
- User asks "how should I word this?"
- User wants an email reviewed for tone

## Email Structure

### Subject Line
- Under 50 characters when possible
- Specific, not vague: "Q3 Budget Review — Action Required" not "Question"
- Include urgency only when genuine: "[Time-Sensitive]" or "[Response Needed by Friday]"

### Opening
- **Cold outreach:** Lead with relevance to them, not about you
- **Reply:** Reference what you're responding to
- **Follow-up:** Reference previous contact and timeline
- **Internal:** Get to the point — no "I hope this email finds you well" in most internal contexts

### Body
- One idea per paragraph
- Bold key actions or deadlines
- Use bullet points for multiple items
- Front-load the ask — don't bury it at the bottom

### Closing
- Clear CTA (call to action): "Could you review by Thursday?"
- Appropriate sign-off matching the relationship

## Templates by Type

### Cold Outreach
```
Subject: [Specific value proposition]

Hi [Name],

[One sentence showing you know their work/company].

[What you're proposing and why it matters to them — 2-3 sentences max].

[Specific, low-commitment ask: "Would a 15-minute call next week work?"]

Best,
[Name]
```

### Follow-Up
```
Subject: Re: [Original subject] — following up

Hi [Name],

Following up on [specific thing] from [date/context].

[Brief restatement of what you need and why].

[New information or adjusted ask if applicable].

Happy to [alternative if they're busy].

Thanks,
[Name]
```

### Rejection (kind but clear)
```
Subject: Re: [Their proposal/request]

Hi [Name],

Thank you for [specific thing they did/proposed].

After [careful consideration / discussing with the team], we've decided to [go a different direction / not move forward at this time].

[One sentence of genuine, specific appreciation — not generic].

[Optional: leave the door open if appropriate].

Best regards,
[Name]
```

## Tone Guide

| Context | Tone | Example Closing |
|---------|------|----------------|
| Executive / C-suite | Formal, concise | "Best regards," |
| Colleague | Professional, warm | "Thanks," or "Best," |
| Client (new) | Professional, attentive | "Looking forward to hearing from you," |
| Client (established) | Warm, direct | "Talk soon," |
| Cold outreach | Respectful, value-first | "Best," |
| Apology | Sincere, no over-explaining | "Thank you for your patience," |

## Guidelines

- Shorter is almost always better. If an email is over 5 paragraphs, it should probably be a meeting
- Never write angry. Draft it, wait, edit, then send
- One email = one ask. Multiple asks get lost
- "Per my last email" is passive-aggressive. Just re-state the point directly
- Read the email from the recipient's perspective before sending: what will they do with this?
- For sensitive topics, suggest the user consider a call instead of email — tone is hard to convey in text
